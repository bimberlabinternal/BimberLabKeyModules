Published Macaque Models:

Macaques serve as essential pre-clinical models for a range of human diseases. Research in macaques has been essential for advances in genetics, immunology, endocrinology, reproductive biology, stem cell biology, biological imaging, and neuroscience. For the study of genetic disorders, the close evolutionary history of macaques and humans is a major advantage, and evident in their highly similar breadth of natural disease susceptibilities.

There are many examples of parallel genetic associations already reported for common diseases, which suggests opportunity to leverage the macaque for developing pharmacogenomic or personalized medicine approaches (Table 1, top). For example, genetic associations in macaques that are orthologous to those associated with human, have been reported for cancers, reproductive disorders, retinal and infectious diseases. Similarly, complex behavioral traits such as heightened anxiety, and variable response to commonly used pharmacological agents such as naltrexone, have been linked to the variants in the same genes as found in humans. Other macaque disease models, such as for autism, polycystic ovarian syndrome (PCOS) or type II diabetes, have been reported, but genetic linkages have yet to be uncovered (Table 1, bottom). The growing list of macaque traits are likely the ‘tip of the iceberg’ of the natural models of human disease, as other diseases likely go undetected either due to a less obvious phenotype (e.g., hearing loss) or due to early life lethality. Together, the broad spectrum of natural models in rhesus macaque populations presents largely untapped opportunities for translational study, and may negate the need for more costly model development approaches, such as gene-editing.
